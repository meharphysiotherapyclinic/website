Tailbone Pain (Coccydynia): Causes, Symptoms, and Physiotherapy Solutions

Sitting comfortably is something most people take for granted—until doing so triggers an unbearable, localized ache at the very base of the spine. If you find yourself constantly shifting weight or leaning forward to avoid touching the back of your chair, you are likely suffering from Coccydynia, commonly known as tailbone pain. This condition can severely limit your ability to work at a desk, commute, or enjoy meals with your family.

1. What Triggers Tailbone Pain?

The coccyx is a small, triangular bone structure at the bottom of your spinal column. It serves as an attachment site for various tendons, ligaments, and deep pelvic floor muscles. Coccydynia occurs when these structures become inflamed, misaligned, or structurally strained. Common causes include:

  • Direct Trauma: A backward slip and fall directly onto your seat, which can bruise, subluxate, or even fracture the small segments of the coccyx.
  • Repetitive Microtrauma: Prolonged, continuous sitting on hard, narrow, or unsupportive chairs—very common among office professionals and long-distance drivers.
  • Pelvic Floor Hypertonicity: Chronic muscle tightness or spasms within the pelvic floor pulling directly on the tailbone joint.
  • Pregnancy and Childbirth: Structural changes and rapid mechanical stretching of the pelvic ring during delivery can push the coccyx beyond its natural range of motion.

For a detailed anatomical overvew of this skeletal structure and clinical diagnosis metrics, you can refer to the Cleveland Clinic's medical guide on Coccydynia (Tailbone Pain).

2. Why Sitting Up Straight Can Sometimes Hurt More

A frequent paradox with coccydynia is that typical "good posture"—sitting perfectly upright at a 90-degree angle—actually forces more direct vertical pressure onto the tailbone, amplifying the sharp pain. Many patients unconsciously adapt by slouching forward or sitting unevenly on one hip, which can inadvertently trigger secondary lower back aches or piriformis syndrome.

3. How Physiotherapy Targets Coccydynia

At Mehar Physiotherapy Clinic, we don't just treat the pain symptoms; we look at the entire pelvic and lumbo-sacral mechanics to correct the root movement flaws. Your customized recovery path includes:

  • Manual Therapy: Specialized, external joint mobilization techniques to safely reposition a subluxated or stiff coccyx and reduce structural strain.
  • Myofascial Release: Gentle release of tight surrounding ligaments and deep gluteal/pelvic muscles to relieve the constant pull on the tailbone.
  • Ergonomic Modifications & Biofeedback: Guidance on immediate offloading strategies, such as using specialized coccyx cut-out wedge cushions to keep the bone suspended and pressure-free.
  • Targeted Dynamic Stretches: Exercises focusing on stretching the piriformis, iliopsoas, and hamstring muscle chains to relieve overall tension across the sacrum.

Because the tailbone area is inherently small and contains complex nerve paths, letting it heal without guidance often leads to chronic inflammation that resists standard painkillers. Early conservative management yields rapid, highly reliable recovery outcomes.
